# Maya Patel — CHRO at a mid-size enterprise SaaS company

> "I sit through QBRs where my CRO walks in with a dashboard tied to closed revenue. I walk in with engagement scores and time-to-hire. We're not having the same conversation."

## Demographics & context

- **Role:** Chief Human Resources Officer
- **Age range:** 45-55
- **Life stage:** Mid-career executive, often a parent of teens or young adults
- **Professional context:** 2,200-person enterprise SaaS company, reports directly to CEO, sits on the executive team. Has a People Analytics lead reporting to her plus 6 HRBPs.
- **Technical proficiency:** High HRIS literacy (Workday, BambooHR, Lattice). Comfortable reading dashboards; less comfortable building them. Has at least one direct report who is "the spreadsheet person."

## Goals & motivations

What Maya is trying to achieve:

1. **Earn a credible seat at the QBR table.** She wants people data that her CFO and CRO actually engage with, not nod through.
2. **Close the 85/87 trust gap.** Her CEO doesn't believe productivity is happening in hybrid; her employees feel surveilled when she tries to prove it is. She wants a defensible answer that's not surveillance.
3. **Move from activity metrics to outcome metrics** before her board asks why she hasn't.

What success looks like to her:

> "Walking into a board meeting with a slide that shows the link between people decisions and revenue, the way finance has shown the link between cost and revenue for thirty years."

What motivates her to look for a solution right now:

The board's last meeting included a 20-minute discussion on whether the company should mandate return-to-office "to fix productivity." She left the meeting having defended hybrid on principle, but without the data to actually prove the case. She has 90 days before the next board meeting.

## Current behavior

How Maya handles this problem today:

- Quarterly engagement survey (Culture Amp). Outputs a 60-page deck. Three slides matter; the rest are submitted to the board as appendix.
- Manager pulse via Lattice. Read by managers, not by Maya.
- Hours worked from Workday timesheets and Zoom call durations. She knows these are activity metrics, not productivity metrics. Uses them anyway because they're what she has.
- Asks her analyst to "pull the data" for specific board questions. The analyst spends 6 hours per ask building a one-off SQL pull. The data answer arrives a day after Maya needed it.

Tools she already uses:

- **Workday** — HRIS of record. Source of truth for headcount, comp, time off.
- **Culture Amp** — engagement survey + 360 reviews. Quarterly cadence.
- **Lattice** — performance management + 1:1 templates. Daily use by managers.
- **Slack** + **Zoom** — collaboration platforms. Sometimes referenced for "how many meetings."
- **Excel** + **Looker** — wherever the actual analysis happens.

Where she spends time online and how she discovers solutions:

- **LinkedIn** — reads Josh Bersin, David Green, Ian Cook religiously. Posts thoughtfully maybe twice a month.
- **CHRO peer groups** — paid memberships at HR Policy Association, Conference Board Council on People Strategy. Where she actually buys what she buys.
- **Workday Rising, HR Tech, Gartner ReimagineHR** — three conferences a year. Two are mandatory; one is for vendor evaluation.
- **Word of mouth from peer CHROs** — the single highest-trust channel. A peer CHRO saying "we use X and it changed things" matters more than a Gartner Magic Quadrant.

## Buying criteria

What matters most to Maya in a solution:

1. **Outcomes, not hours.** Anything that pitches keystroke counts or attention tracking dies in the first 60 seconds of the demo.
2. **Connects people data to business data.** Can it tie engagement, attrition, or org structure to revenue, customer outcomes, or product velocity? If yes, she leans in. If no, it's a vanity tool.
3. **Board-credible output.** Can the CFO and CEO read a slide from this tool and believe it? Not "is it accurate," but "does it look like the kind of analysis they trust from other functions."
4. **Defensible privacy posture.** GDPR, AI Act, NY Local Law 144 ready out of the box. Auditable consent flows. Employee transparency by default.
5. **Doesn't require her analyst to learn a new query language.** Time-to-insight matters more than dashboard customization.

Deal-breakers:

- Anything described as "employee monitoring" in the website copy
- Anything that requires installing client-side software on every employee laptop
- "Trust us, the AI is fair" with no audit trail
- A sales process longer than 6 weeks (she has a 90-day board window)

Budget considerations and decision-making process:

- Discretionary budget: $200K-500K for a tool of this kind without going to the CFO
- Above $500K: requires CFO sign-off and CEO endorsement
- Procurement cycle: 8-12 weeks for tools above $100K
- Typically pilots with one business unit (Engineering, usually) before company-wide rollout

## Objections & barriers

Why Maya might NOT buy:

1. **"Show me a peer CHRO who uses you, by name, who I can actually call."** No reference, no sale. Logos on a website don't count.
2. **"What happens at audit?"** If she can't show the People Analytics function's data lineage and the privacy posture to an auditor, she can't sign.
3. **"We already pay for [Visier/Workday Adaptive/Tableau]. Why does this exist?"** She is genuinely tired of dashboard tools and needs a clear answer to why this is different.
4. **"Will my employees freak out?"** Internal comms risk is real. Anything that smells like surveillance gets killed by her own communications team.

Past experiences with similar solutions:

She bought into Glint (now part of LinkedIn) early. The data was useful for two quarters, then she realized the manager dashboards stopped getting opened. She spent $180K on a tool whose engagement died at the manager layer. She's not making that mistake again.
